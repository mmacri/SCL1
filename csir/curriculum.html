Seattle City Light • OT Cyber Security Incident Response Plan

Guided CSIR training built from the SCL plan

The CSIR curriculum teaches the SCL OT Cyber Security Incident Response Plan (CSIRP) for OT cyber assets such as BES cyber assets, ECAMS, and PACS managed by SCL. It does not cover systems solely run by Seattle IT.

  • Understand the plan’s purpose, scope, and how it improves resilience.
  • Know role responsibilities, communications, and reporting expectations.
  • Use CSIR terminology, incident categories, and severity correctly.
  • Work the six-phase workflow, document evidence, and meet deadlines.

CSIR workflow spine

Preparation → Identification → Investigation → Response → Recovery → Follow-up

Every module maps to one of the six phases. Start at the left and move right with a single, continuous runbook.

Preparation

Keep plans current, confirm monitoring/logging, and stage backups/images.

Investigation

Response

Contain, eradicate, and document actions with approvals.

Recovery

Restore safely, validate systems, and prevent recurrence.

Follow-up

Module 1

CSIR Overview

Purpose, goals, scope, and assumptions that keep the CSIR plan operational.

Purpose & goals

Increase security and resilience across OT networks and critical infrastructure, decreasing illicit cyber activity and improving incident response. Aligned to SCL’s cyber security master policy and NERC CIP standards (CIP‑007‑6, CIP‑008‑6, CIP‑009‑6).

Threats & failures

  • Malware or unauthorized access
  • OT outage and loss of monitoring
  • Credential misuse or tampering

CSIR outcomes

  • Resilient operations and safety
  • Coordinated incident response
  • Regulatory compliance

Scope & applicability

SCL OT CSIR
OT Systems
Seattle IT managed
  • In scope: BES cyber assets, ECAMS, PACS, and other SCL OT systems.
  • Out of scope: systems managed solely by Seattle IT for SCL.
  • Assumptions: trained stakeholders, current contacts, accurate asset inventories, recovery plans, backups, and spares maintained by system owners.

Module 3

Terminology, Classification, Severity

Core definitions

Cyber Event – unusual activity (e.g., benign scan).
Cyber Security Incident – malicious/suspicious act impacting a cyber asset.
Attempt to Compromise – suspicious activity with little/no immediate impact; still critical.
Reportable Cyber Security Incident – meets mandatory criteria and deadlines.
Event → Incident → AtC → Reportable Incident

Shows how terms overlap as impact and reportability rise.

Incident categories

  • Malicious Code
  • Intrusion Incident
  • Denial of Service
  • Reconnaissance
  • Unauthorized Access
  • Phishing
  • Other

Severity levels

Critical: cannot provide critical services; significant interruption to electrical power operations; inability to operate/monitor systems; utility‑wide data loss.
High: moderate interruption of critical services; business continuity at risk; data corruption; active malicious activity on critical systems.
Medium: loss of critical service to subset; potential impact to adequacy/reliability; detected precursor.
Low: minimal effect; services available with reduced efficiency; recon/suspicious activity; malware on non‑critical systems.

Phase goals

  • Preparation: maintain plan distribution, monitoring, logging, backups.
  • Identification: classify quickly, assign severity, start documentation.
  • Investigation: confirm scope, expand notifications, refine decisions.
  • Response: contain, eradicate, coordinate ICS and reporting.
  • Recovery: restore validated systems safely with spares/backups.
  • Follow-Up: lessons learned, documentation updates, monitoring.

Swimlane snapshot

Incident Commander: approvals, escalation, messaging.
Technical Lead: classification, scope, containment options.
ECS: reporter and evidence steward.
OT Ops: execute containment/recovery with safety first.
Compliance: align to reporting and audit artifacts.

Micro-scenario: advance through phases, deciding when to escalate or continue investigating. Feedback references the workflow tables.

Reporting & Documentation

Reporting decision tree

Documentation & evidence

  • Record all incidents in the Cyber Security Incident Log (Tier 2 included).
  • Retention: final reports and data ≥7 years; logs/evidence ≥3 years or longest requirement.
  • ECS retains evidence and provides copies for NERC CIP audits.

Documentation checklist

  • Time of detection and classification
  • Affected systems and severity
  • Actions taken and by whom
  • Reporting timestamps
  • Lessons learned / follow-up items
Initial report (≤1 hr) Update (≤7 days) Follow-up (≤90 days)
Module 6

OT Scenarios & Drills

Hands-on drills replace generic decision tables.

Scenario layout

  1. Context: OT system diagram + story (e.g., suspicious logins on substation gateway).
  2. Identification: event vs incident, initial severity, notify who.
  3. Investigation: logs to review, systems to isolate, AtC/reportable decision.
  4. Response: containment options and ICS coordination.
  5. Recovery: move from incident to recovery, restore from backups.
  6. Follow-Up: documentation artifacts and lessons learned.

Scoring focus

  • Classification & severity accuracy mapped to CSIR tables.
  • Correct reporting and timing (1-hour initial, 7-day updates, 90-day follow-up).
  • Role-appropriate actions (All Staff report; Technical Lead refines and leads technical work).
  • Evidence completeness and documentation quality.
